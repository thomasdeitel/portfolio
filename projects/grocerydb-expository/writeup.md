# GroceryDB Expository Write-up

I wanted the chart to answer a simple question: does the store you shop at really change how processed your options are? The main pattern that I found was that the answer is yes in some parts of the store, but not in others. Whole Foods does have a less processed assortment overall, but interestingly, the biggest differences show up in categories like beans, seafood, packaged produce, and meat. So, once you get into categories like cookies, chips, bread, and cakes, all three stores start to look very similar because almost everything is already ultra-processed.

To make the comparison more fair, I turned the four class NOVA style prediction into one number per category which was the share of items labeled ultra-processed (`FPro_class = 3`). I also filtered the main chart to categories with at least 75 items in Whole Foods, Walmart, and Target, so then I was not comparing a large aisle in one store to a tiny sample in another business or place. Then, that left 20 categories and 17,999 products. I sorted the rows by average ultra processed share, which creates a top to bottom progression from fresher categories to categories that are just more processed almost across the board.

My checkpoint version used a heatmap, but for the final I switched to a position based chart because it is way easier to compare percentages by location on a shared axis instead rather by color intensity. Each row shows the three stores as colored dots, and then the gray line between the outer points to show how far apart the stores are within that indiviudal category. I kept the summary cards at the top to give quick overall context, then used the background shading and callouts to then show the reader to the main point of it. The side notes quantify that pattern that categories below 80% are ultra processed have a much larger typical store gap than categories that are already above 90%.

This chart focuses on an assortment mix, so it leaves some things out on purpose. It does not show the prices, nutrients, or brand level differences, and it counts each product once instead of weighting by shelf space or sales interestingly. I also dropped my earlier price per calorie idea because it depended more heavily on missing values and the outliers and I thought it was easier to then over interpret. One category that does not cleanly fit the overall pattern is nuts and seeds which is why I left it visible instead of just smoothing it away.
